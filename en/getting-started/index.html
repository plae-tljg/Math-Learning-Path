Getting Started with Math

Welcome! Here I’ll try to share some of my thoughts as someone who has studied math. I hope it helps you figure out your own attitude towards the subject—whether you’re here to learn, question, or just be curious.

What is math?

If you look up mathematics on Wikipedia, here’s the standard explanation:

Mathematics is a field of study that discovers and organizes methods, theories, and theorems that are developed and proved for the needs of empirical sciences and mathematics itself. There are many areas of mathematics, which include:
  • Number theory: the study of numbers
  • Algebra: the study of formulas and related structures
  • Geometry: the study of shapes and the spaces that contain them
  • Analysis: the study of continuous changes
  • Set theory: presently used as a foundation for all mathematics

To be honest, this kind of definition always sounded a bit hollow to me. Sure, math as a tool is incredibly practical—it’s everywhere, in engineering, science, and daily life. But when people praise mathematics purely for its abstract theories, or treat it like it’s some perfect kingdom of logical beauty, I can’t help but feel a little skeptical.

Pure math—the side obsessed with proofs, structures, or concepts far removed from anything I’ll ever encounter in reality—often seems pointless or even exhausting. It can feel like mental gymnastics for their own sake, disconnected from the real world. In my experience, the parts of math I actually appreciate are the ones with useful applications: when it helps you solve problems, makes things work, or shows up unexpectedly in technology or daily tasks.

Maybe you feel the same frustration, or maybe you love theory for its own sake. But it’s important to acknowledge that it’s okay not to find every aspect of math magical or inspiring. Math can be challenging, dull, or just plain annoying—especially when it’s presented as an abstract subject with no clear relevance.

How can you start learning math?

My best advice? Start by focusing on the math that actually solves problems you care about. Here are some practical tips:

  • Look for the real-world side. Use math to answer questions or accomplish tasks—like budgeting, planning, or analyzing data—rather than getting bogged down in abstract theory.
  • Don’t buy the hype. You’re not alone if you find some branches of math tedious or irrelevant; focus on what matters to you.
  • Break things down. Complicated concepts can often be made manageable with a bit of patience and by connecting them to things you already know.
  • It’s fine to dislike some of it. Don’t force yourself to enjoy everything—just try to find the bits that are useful or (for you) interesting.
  • Talk about it openly. Being honest about what you find boring or frustrating is much healthier (and often more productive!) than pretending to be endlessly fascinated.

In short: If you want to use math as a tool, don’t worry about not loving it for its own sake. Use it where it helps, and don’t feel bad when you’d rather spend your time on something else.

Where should you go next?

If you want to keep learning, I recommend:

  • Applied math resources: Check out guides and websites that emphasize practical usage—like statistics, coding, or basic finance.
  • Math in context: Look for sources that explain why and where math matters, not just how to prove theorems in a vacuum. You might find math more tolerable (or even enjoyable) when it’s actually doing something useful.
  • Skip what you dislike: It’s perfectly fine to ignore pure math or advanced abstractions if you don’t see the point.

You don’t have to be a math fan to appreciate its uses. Find whatever helps you get things done. And if you ever want to rant about how annoying pure math can be, know that you’re definitely not alone.

Thanks for reading—and good luck finding your own way with math, whatever that ends up meaning for you.
